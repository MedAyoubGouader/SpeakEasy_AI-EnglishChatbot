ENGLISH PRONUNCIATION GUIDE (A1-C2)

=== VOWEL SOUNDS (Critical for English) ===

SHORT VOWELS (Simple, quick sounds)

/æ/ (cat, apple, bad)
- Sound: Between /a/ and /e/
- French equivalent: Slightly open /a/
- Words: cat, bag, and, black, map, path, laugh
- Practice: Apple is red

/ɪ/ (sit, with, big)
- Sound: Short /i/
- French equivalent: Similar to French /i/ but shorter
- Words: sit, big, bit, with, will, kill, fish
- Practice: This is big

/ɒ/ (British /ɑ/ American) (not, pot, dog)
- Sound: Open /o/
- French equivalent: /o/ but more open
- Words: not, pot, dog, stop, clock, shot, on
- Practice: The dog sat on the pot

/ʌ/ (cup, but, love)
- Sound: Between /a/ and /o/ (central)
- French equivalent: Difficult for French speakers, practice!
- Words: cup, but, love, some, come, up, fun
- Practice: I love a cup of tea

/ə/ (about, sofa, the)
- Sound: Unstressed, relaxed sound
- Frequency: Most common sound in English!
- Words: about, sofa, the (before consonant), across
- Practice: (the) cat = /ðə kæt/
- Note: Schwa appears in unstressed syllables

LONG VOWELS (Hold the sound longer)

/iː/ (see, tree, key)
- Sound: Long /i/
- Compare: sit /ɪ/ vs sea /iː/
- Words: see, tree, key, feet, meat, green, be
- Practice: He can see the green tree

/uː/ (moon, food, blue)
- Sound: Long /u/
- Compare: foot /ʊ/ vs food /uː/
- Words: moon, food, blue, too, who, knew, soup
- Practice: The food is good

/ɔː/ (British) /ɔ/ (American) (door, four, call)
- Sound: Long /o/
- Words: door, four, call, ball, small, thought, board
- Practice: The ball is on the floor

/ɑː/ (British) /ɑ/ (American) (car, dark, father)
- Sound: Long /a/
- Words: car, dark, father, dance, last, smile, glass
- Practice: Father has a dark car

/ɜː/ (British) /ɜ/ (American) (bird, girl, work)
- Sound: Central long sound
- Words: bird, girl, work, word, nurse, turn, hurt
- Practice: The nurse works at the hospital

DIPHTHONGS (Two vowel sounds gliding together)

/eɪ/ (day, make, play)
- Sounds: /e/ → /ɪ/
- Words: day, make, play, cake, face, name, same
- Practice: Make a cake for my birthday

/aɪ/ (time, like, my)
- Sounds: /a/ → /ɪ/
- Words: time, like, my, fly, high, smile, white
- Practice: I like to fly

/ɔɪ/ (boy, coin, oil)
- Sounds: /o/ → /ɪ/
- Words: boy, coin, oil, enjoy, voice, choice, point
- Practice: The boy enjoys the coin

/aʊ/ (house, now, cow)
- Sounds: /a/ → /ʊ/
- Words: house, now, cow, down, about, sound, mouth
- Practice: The cow is around the house

/ə/ + /ʊ/ (go, show, so)
- Sounds: /ə/ → /ʊ/
- Words: go, show, so, cold, know, old, phone, home
- Practice: I go home now

/ɪ/ + /ə/ (here, near, year)
- Sounds: /ɪ/ → /ə/
- Words: here, near, year, clear, hear, fear, dear
- Practice: I hear the deer here

/ɛ/ + /ə/ (where, care, fair)
- Sounds: /ɛ/ → /ə/
- Words: where, care, fair, hair, there, share, spare
- Practice: Where is the fair?

/ɔː/ + /ə/ (door, more, four)
- Sounds: /ɔː/ → /ə/
- Words: door, more, four, store, course, board, pour
- Practice: There are four more doors

=== CONSONANT SOUNDS ===

DIFFICULT CONSONANTS FOR FRENCH SPEAKERS

/θ/ (think, thin, bath)
- Sound: Unvoiced, tongue between teeth
- French: No equivalent - PRACTICE!
- Position: Tongue tip between teeth
- Words: think, thin, thank, thing, bath, mouth, north
- Practice: "Think thin" /θɪŋk θɪn/
- Contrast: Think /θɪŋk/ vs Sin /sɪn/

/ð/ (this, that, the)
- Sound: Voiced version of /θ/
- Tongue: Between teeth, voice on
- Words: this, that, the, these, those, with, smooth
- Practice: This is that thing
- Contrast: This /ðɪs/ vs Dis /dɪs/

/ŋ/ (sing, ring, thing)
- Sound: Velar nasal, back of throat
- Position: Back of mouth, not /n/
- Words: sing, ring, thing, coming, running, long
- Practice: "Singing ring" /ˈsɪŋɪŋ rɪŋ/
- Common error: NOT /sɪn/ (sin)

/w/ (we, water, win)
- Sound: Voiced, rounded lips
- Position: Lips rounded, moving to next vowel
- Words: we, water, win, well, want, away, new
- Practice: "We want water"
- NOT: /v/ (very different!)

/j/ (yes, yellow, year)
- Sound: Semi-vowel, like French /j/
- Position: Hard palate
- Words: yes, yellow, year, young, music, used
- Practice: "Yes, I'm young"

/r/ (red, right, run)
- Sound: Alveolar approximant (NOT rolled!)
- Position: Slightly back, NOT trilled like French
- Words: red, right, run, car, door, three
- Practice: "Right red car"
- Note: British English /r/ only before vowels

=== STRESS & INTONATION ===

WORD STRESS (Which syllable is louder)

PRIMARY STRESS PATTERNS

One-syllable words (no stress confusion)
- CAT, TREE, HOUSE, WATER has stress on first syllable

Two-syllable words
- Pattern 1 (Stress on 1st): APple, HEPpy, MONey, TAble, CIty
- Pattern 2 (Stress on 2nd): beHIND, enJOY, hoTEL, prepARE
- Rule of thumb: More nouns stress 1st syllable; verbs often 2nd

Three-syllable words
- Pattern: BOTany, beTWEEN, proTECtion, eXAMple
- Common: uNIversity, caPAcity, proBLEM

Four+ syllable words
- English: conTINue, conMUNicate, oPORtunity, inFORmation
- Usually 1st or 2nd syllable

STRESS CHANGES WORD MEANING
- REcord (noun) vs reCORD (verb)
- PREsent (noun) vs preSENT (verb)
- CONtest (noun) vs conTEST (verb)
- OBject (noun) vs obJECT (verb)
- PERmit (noun) vs perMIT (verb)

BRITISH vs AMERICAN STRESS
- British: ADvertisement vs American: adVERtisement
- British: DIrectly vs American: diRECTly
- British: SCHED-ule vs American: SKED-ule

SENTENCE STRESS (Information focus)

Content words (stressed): Nouns, verbs, adjectives, adverbs
Function words (unstressed): Articles, prepositions, pronouns, auxiliaries

Example: "I LOVE drinking COFFEE in the MORning"
- Stressed: LOVE, COFFEE, MORNING
- Unstressed: I, in, the

CHANGING STRESS FOR EMPHASIS
Normal: "I THINK it's WONderful"
Emphasis: "I think it's WONderful" (disbelief tone)

INTONATION PATTERNS

FALLING INTONATION (↘)
- Use: Statements, commands, questions with question words
- Example: "What's your NAME?" (falls on name)
- Pattern: Pitch drops at end

RISING INTONATION (↗)
- Use: Yes/no questions, uncertainty, continuation
- Example: "You like COFfee?" (rises on coffee = question)
- Pattern: Pitch rises at end

RISE-FALL INTONATION (↗↘)
- Use: Emphatic statements, surprise
- Example: "That's SMAzing!" (rises then falls dramatically)

=== CONNECTED SPEECH ===

LINKING (Connecting sounds)

Consonant → Vowel linking
- Example: "not [a] / often" = /nɒt ɒ əɔːfən/
- Practice: "put it on" = "puditOn"

Same consonant / vowel linking
- Example: "big guy" = "bigguy" (one long sound)
- Practice: "that thing" = "ðæt θɪŋ" (sounds natural)

ELISION (Sounds disappearing)

Consonant clusters reduced
- "Next day" = /neks deɪ/ (drop /t/)
- "Acts" = /ækts/ (drop short vowel in cluster)

Unstressed vowels (schwa) disappearing
- "Every" = "EVRY" (not "EVeryee")
- "Camera" = "CAMRA" (not "CAmeraah")

ASSIMILATION (Sounds changing)

Common assimilations
- "Did you" = "didja" /dɪdʒə/
- "Want to" = "wanna" /ˈwɑːnə/ (informal)
- "Have to" = "hafta" /ˈhæftə/ (informal)
- "Got you" = "gotcha" /ˈɡɑːtʃə/

=== BRITISH vs AMERICAN PRONUNCIATION ===

MAJOR DIFFERENCES

Vowel /r/ pronunciation
- British: /r/ only before vowels (car = /kɑː/)
- American: /r/ always pronounced (car = /kɑːr/)

/ɑː/ vs /æ/
- British: "can't" = /kɑːnt/ (long a)
- American: "can't" = /kænt/ (short a)

/əʊ/ vs /oʊ/
- British: "go" = /ɡəʊ/ (glide from schwa)
- American: "go" = /ɡoʊ/ (more rounded)

T pronunciation
- British: /t/ (butter = /ˈbʌtə/)
- American: /ɾ/ flapped (butter = /ˈbʌɾɚ/)

=== PRONUNCIATION PRACTICE WORDS ===

EASY (A1)
- Sat /sæt/, sit /sɪt/, set /set/
- Hot /hɑt/, hat /hæt/, hit /hɪt/
- How /haʊ/, high /haɪ/, hey /heɪ/

MEDIUM (A2)
- Though /ðoʊ/, through /θruː/, throw /θroʊ/
- Which /wɪtʃ/, witch /wɪtʃ/, wish /wɪʃ/
- Pronunciation /prəˌnʌnsiˈeɪʃən/

ADVANCED (B1+)
- Worcestershire /ˈwʊstərʃər/ (UK county)
- Sargent /ˈsɑːrdʒənt/ vs sergeant /ˈsɑːrdʒənt/ (same!)
- Clothes /kloʊðz/, months /mʌnθs/, rhythm /ˈrɪðəm/

=== COMMON MISPRONOUNCED WORDS ===

- OFTEN: /ˈɒfən/ or /ˈɔːfən/ (NOT /ˈɒft/)
- ISLAND: /ˈaɪlənd/ (s is SILENT)
- DEBRIS: /dəˈbriː/ (NOT /ˈdebri/)
- WEDNESDAY: /ˈwenzdeɪ/ (NOT /wednz/)
- LIBRARY: /ˈlaɪbreri/ (NOT /ˈlibrary/)
- PROBABLY: /ˈprɑːbəbli/ (NOT /ˈprobs/)
- USUALLY: /ˈjuːʒuəli/ (NOT /usually/)
- FEBRUARY: /ˈfebrueri/ (NOT /fΔbwuary/)
- SCHEDULE: British /ˈʃedʒuːl/, American /ˈskedʒuːl/
- VICTUALS: /ˈvɪtəlz/ (NOT /vik-choo-alz/)

=== ACCENT TRAINING ===

NATIVE-LIKE PRONUNCIATION

1. Listen actively (movies, podcasts, songs)
2. Copy the rhythm and stress patterns
3. Record yourself and compare
4. Focus on weak forms (the, a, are)
5. Practice connected speech
6. Exaggerate mouth movements
7. Use mirror to check tongue position

DAILY PRACTICE TIPS
- Record 1-2 new words daily
- Practice minimal pairs (sit/seat, ship/sheep)
- Listen to audiobooks with transcript
- Shadow English speakers (repeat while listening)
